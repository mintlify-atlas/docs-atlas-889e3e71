## Introduction

The Indo-Pacific Humpback Dolphin is best known in Thailand as the Pink Dolphin (โลมาชมพู). Its most striking feature is a color change that unfolds over the animal's lifetime. Juveniles are dark grey to black. As they mature, the color fades and patches of white appear. In older adults, the body becomes entirely white or develops a distinct pink flush — the result of blood vessels close to the skin surface rather than any pink pigmentation.

The dolphin has a rounded head, a long and slender beak, and 28–38 teeth in each jaw. It is a social, coastal species that lives in small groups and remains in the same general home range throughout its life.

In Thailand, the Khanom–Don Sak Bay area in the Gulf of Thailand is the most well-known habitat for this species, and it has become a tourist destination for dolphin-watching boat trips.

## Threats

<Warning>
  **Human-caused threats:**
  - **Irresponsible tourism** — Tour boats in Khanom Bay have practiced feeding wild dolphins. Feeding changes natural behavior and exposes animals to contaminated food. This is actively campaigned against by conservation authorities.
  - **Bycatch** — Dolphins become accidentally entangled in fishing gear including gill nets and illegal fishing equipment.
  - **Vessel strikes** — Propeller injuries in feeding areas can be fatal.
  - **Marine pollution** — Plastic ingestion causes digestive blockages. Toxins in wastewater accumulate in the body over time.

  **Natural threats:**
  - Illness, age-related weakness, and predation by sharks or orcas can cause stranding.
</Warning>

## Conservation solutions

<Tip>
  - All dolphins and whales in Thai waters are protected under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2535, with absolute bans on hunting, trading, and possession.
  - CITES Appendix I listing prohibits all international trade.
  - Conservation campaigns specifically target **dolphin feeding**, particularly in Khanom Bay, to restore natural foraging behavior.
  - Reducing marine litter is a key long-term action — plastic debris is one of the most widespread threats to all marine megafauna.
</Tip>

### Stranding response

When a Pink Dolphin is found stranded alive:

1. Support the animal in water, keeping the blowhole above the surface. Never cover the blowhole.
2. If the animal is on shore, dig depressions under the pectoral fins to reduce pressure on organs. Cover the body (not the blowhole or dorsal fin) with wet cloth and pour water continuously to keep the skin moist.
3. Transport using a correctly-sized canvas stretcher to avoid additional wounds.
4. Transfer to a rehabilitation pool wide enough for the dolphin to swim freely. Veterinarians diagnose and treat until the animal is ready for release.
