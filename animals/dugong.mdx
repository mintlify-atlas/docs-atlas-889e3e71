## Introduction

The Dugong is a marine mammal that shares a common ancestor with the elephant from roughly 55 million years ago, before it evolved into a fully aquatic life and never returned to land. Its body is torpedo-shaped like a dolphin's but rounder, with no dorsal fin, a large fluked tail, and thick, greyish-pink to brownish-grey skin that is paler on the belly. Short sparse hairs cover the body, while coarser bristles cluster around the mouth, which it uses to graze on seagrass.

The Dugong's head features small eyes and ear openings without external pinnae. Valve-lined nostrils allow it to surface and breathe every one to two minutes. A heavy, dense skeleton helps it sink to the seafloor to feed.

Dugongs eat seagrass — including species like *Halophila ovalis* and *Cymodocea rotundata* — at a rate of 2–5% of body weight per day. Despite having no natural weapons, they rely on their large size, tough hide, and fast-clotting blood for protection.

Age can be estimated by counting growth layers in the tusks, which erupt in males at around 13–14 years. Females can be distinguished by mammary glands below the pectoral fins and a genital opening close to the anal opening.

Dugongs live up to 70 years and are found on both the Gulf of Thailand coast (Chonburi, Rayong, Chanthaburi, Trat, Chumphon, Surat Thani, Pattani) and the Andaman coast. The single largest concentration in Thailand is at Libong and Muk islands in Trang province, which historically accounts for 49% of all recorded strandings nationally.

## Threats

<Warning>
  **Human-caused threats:**
  - **Bycatch** — Dugongs share feeding grounds with fishing areas and regularly become accidentally entangled in gill nets, illegal trawls, blast-fishing debris, and electrofishing gear.
  - **Coastal structures** — Bamboo wave-break fences and other coastal engineering can trap Dugongs feeding in shallow water during low tide, preventing them from returning to deeper water.
  - **Marine pollution** — Debris ingestion, wastewater contamination, and toxin accumulation through the food chain cause illness and death.
  - **Noise pollution** — Seismic surveys for petroleum and fishing vessel sonar disrupt the Dugong's communication and navigation.
  - **Poaching** — Post-mortem examinations have documented cut tusk tips, indicating deliberate mutilation for trade.
  - **Vessel strikes** — Boat propellers injure or kill Dugongs in feeding areas.

  **Natural threats:**
  - Sharks and orcas are natural predators. When pursued, Dugongs can panic-swim into shallow water and strand, or develop decompression sickness from rapid ascent.
  - Storms, strong currents, and rapid tidal drops strand shallow-water feeders.
  - Natural illness.
</Warning>

## Conservation solutions

<Tip>
  The Dugong is one of Thailand's most strictly protected marine animals.

  - Listed as a **reserved protected species** under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2562, with severe penalties for hunting, trading, or possession.
  - Listed on CITES **Appendix I** — all international trade is prohibited.
  - Certain fishing gear is banned in designated coastal zones to reduce bycatch risk.
  - The DMCR operates an online incident-reporting system at **mes.dmcr.go.th** for public sightings and strandings.
</Tip>

### Response protocol for live strandings

When a Dugong strands alive, the standard response follows Thai DMCR guidelines:

1. **Report** — Notify the conservation network immediately so trained personnel and veterinarians can be dispatched.
2. **Assess** — If the animal is healthy, return it to the sea immediately. If it is injured or distressed (entangled in gear, weak, or wounded), provide first aid and transport it to a rehabilitation pool.
3. **Rehabilitation** — The Dugong is held in a smooth-surfaced recovery pool large enough to allow turning, with water depth calibrated to prevent drowning, under veterinary supervision until ready for release.
4. **Post-mortem** — If the Dugong has died, a necropsy is performed to identify the cause of death — whether human-caused (entanglement, ingestion, assault) or natural — and the data is entered into the national stranding database.
