## Introduction

The Leatherback Turtle is the largest sea turtle in the world. Adults can reach a shell length of 210 cm and weigh up to 900 kg, though most turtles that come ashore to nest are at least 150 cm long.

Unlike all other sea turtles, the Leatherback has no hard, scute-covered shell. Instead, its back is covered by a thick, flexible black skin studded with small white spots and reinforced by seven prominent longitudinal ridges that run from head to tail. The head is also scaleless, and the upper jaw features three distinct notches.

Hatchlings emerge jet black with white-edged scales along the ridges of the body. As they grow, the white markings spread across the entire surface.

Leatherbacks spend their entire lives at sea, feeding near the surface on jellyfish and sponges. They only approach the coast during the breeding season. Females return to nest every one to three years, laying their eggs between December and February. In Thailand, nesting occurs exclusively on mainland beaches in Phang-nga and Phuket — making these beaches irreplaceable for the species' survival.

## Threats

<Warning>
  Leatherback Turtles face threats from both human activities and natural hazards. Human-caused threats are the primary driver of population decline.

  **Human-caused threats:**
  - **Bycatch** — Turtles are accidentally caught in fishing gear including gill nets, trawl nets, and longline hooks.
  - **Plastic ingestion** — Leatherbacks mistake floating plastic bags for jellyfish. Plastic obstructs the digestive tract and causes illness and death.
  - **Water pollution** — Chemical contaminants accumulate in the food chain.
  - **Coastal development** — Seawalls and erosion barriers on Phang-nga and Phuket beaches can block access to nesting sites and trap hatchlings trying to reach the sea.
  - **Vessel strikes** — Boat propellers cause serious or fatal injuries.
  - **Illegal fishing** — Blast fishing and the use of nets in protected areas.

  **Natural threats:**
  - Storms and rapidly changing sea levels can strand turtles and disrupt nesting beaches.
  - Natural predators such as sharks and orcas cause turtles to flee into shallow water, risking stranding.
  - Disease and natural illness.
</Warning>

## Conservation solutions

<Tip>
  Thailand has elevated legal protections for all sea turtles to the highest available level under national law.

  - The Leatherback Turtle is listed as a **reserved protected species** under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2562. Hunting, trading, possessing, or breeding the animal — or its eggs, parts, or carcass — carries severe criminal penalties.
  - Thailand is a signatory to CITES. The Leatherback is listed on **Appendix I**, prohibiting all international commercial trade.
</Tip>

### If you find an entangled or stranded turtle

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Entangled in net">
    Cut away the net fragments and check for wounds. If the turtle is healthy, release it immediately. Never pull forcefully on a fishing line attached to a swallowed hook — this tears the esophagus. Instead, push the hook point through the tissue, clip off the barb, then withdraw the hook the way it entered. If the hook is deep in the throat or stomach, contact authorities or a veterinarian immediately.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Stranded on shore">
    Move the turtle to a shaded, quiet area. Cover the shell with a damp cloth to maintain moisture — do not cover the nostrils. If the turtle is ill, weak, or has serious wounds, transport it using a suitable stretcher (dry, with a wet cloth covering) within four hours. Do not submerge it in water during transport. If you suspect it has inhaled water, elevate the rear of the body above the head to allow fluid to drain from the lungs.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Reporting">
    Contact the Thai Department of Marine and Coastal Resources (DMCR) stranding network. Community members, fishing crews, and tourism operators all participate in the early-warning system.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
