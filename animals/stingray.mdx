## Introduction

The Giant Manta Ray is the largest ray in the world. Its wingspan — the width of its flattened disc body — reaches 7–9 metres. Unlike most rays, the Giant Manta Ray's mouth is at the very front of its head (a terminal mouth position) rather than on the underside. The dorsal surface of the head bears a distinctive black T-shaped band, and the underside is white with grey-black edges and scattered dark spots. These spots are unique to each individual and can be used for photo identification, much like a fingerprint.

At the base of the tail, a small vestigial remnant of a spine is present — this is not a functional stinging spine, and the Giant Manta Ray poses no sting threat.

Giant Manta Rays filter-feed by swimming through concentrations of plankton, small invertebrates, and fish eggs, funneling water through their gill slits. They are long-distance ocean migrants that travel between ocean basins. They regularly visit underwater pinnacles and coral structures that function as **cleaning stations** — sites where small cleaner fish remove parasites from the ray's body surface.

Despite a lifespan of approximately 45 years, Giant Manta Rays have an extremely low reproductive rate: females give birth to just one pup at a time, with a four-to-five year interval between pregnancies. This makes population recovery from depletion very slow.

In Thailand, the species is found in the Gulf of Thailand (rarely) and more commonly in the Andaman Sea at offshore dive sites including Bon Island, Tachai Island, and Richelieu Rock in Phang-nga; Raja Noi Island in Phuket; and Hin Daeng–Hin Muang in Krabi.

## Threats

<Warning>
  The Giant Manta Ray is classified as Endangered on the IUCN Red List and is at risk of continued population decline from several sources:

  **Fishing and gear-related threats:**
  - **Bycatch** — Accidentally caught in commercial purse-seine nets and trawl nets operating in migration corridors.
  - **Ghost gear** — Abandoned or lost fishing equipment (nets, ropes) wraps around the disc, cephalic lobes (horn-like fins near the mouth), or other body parts, causing progressive cutting wounds, infection, and death.

  **Commercial trade:**
  - **Gill rakers** — The filtering structures in the Manta Ray's gills are highly valued in Chinese medicine markets. Demand for gill rakers has driven targeted hunting and significant population declines globally.
  - **Meat** — Manta Ray flesh is consumed in some markets.

  **Irresponsible tourism:**
  - Divers who chase, closely approach, touch, or use flash photography disturb the ray's natural feeding behavior and interfere with its use of cleaning stations, causing the animal to abandon the site.
</Warning>

## Conservation solutions

<Tip>
  - The Giant Manta Ray is a **protected wildlife species** under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2562. Import, export, and possession of the animal or its parts is prohibited without a permit.
  - CITES **Appendix II** listing controls international trade.
  - The DMCR cooperates with the **Marine Megafauna Foundation (MMF)** and citizen science networks of volunteer divers to conduct photo-ID surveys tracking individual rays and cross-border migration.
  - Conservation guidelines for responsible manta ray diving are published and promoted at all major Andaman dive sites.
</Tip>

### If a Manta Ray is entangled in fishing gear

If you encounter a Giant Manta Ray entangled in a net or rope:

1. **Report immediately** — Contact DMCR or local marine park authorities. Intervention by trained divers is required.
2. **Do not attempt solo removal** — Entanglement removal for large animals requires specialized equipment and trained teams.
3. A response team of experienced divers will approach the animal carefully and cut away net material or rope without causing further injury.

### Responsible diving behavior

- Maintain a respectful distance and do not chase or intercept the ray's path.
- Never touch a Manta Ray — surface oils from human hands damage the protective mucus layer.
- No flash photography.
- Do not hover directly above or below the animal.
