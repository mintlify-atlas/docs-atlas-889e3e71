## Introduction

The Sperm Whale is the world's largest toothed predator. Its most defining characteristic is its enormous, box-shaped head — nearly 40% of its total body length — which contains the spermaceti organ, a wax-filled structure thought to play a role in buoyancy control and echolocation.

The body is dark grey to dark brown with a slightly paler underside. The lower jaw is narrow and small relative to the massive head, and carries the whale's teeth — the upper jaw is toothless. Sperm Whales are powerful echolocators, producing low-frequency burst-pulse sounds that are among the loudest biological sounds on Earth, used for navigation and prey detection at depth.

Sperm Whales are highly social animals. Pods maintain exceptionally close bonds between individuals, a trait that is central to both their survival and their vulnerability. When one member of a pod is in distress, other members approach and stay near — a behavior that, when a pod member strands in shallow water, can result in the entire group beaching together.

## Threats

<Warning>
  **Primary threats:**

  - **Mass stranding** — Because Sperm Whales are socially bound, a single navigational error by the pod leader, prey-chase into shallow water, or injury to one individual can cause an entire pod to strand simultaneously. Mass stranding events are among the most challenging rescue situations in marine animal conservation.
  - **Plastic and marine debris** — Documented cases include dwarf sperm whales that died after ingesting large quantities of plastic bags, which compacted in the stomach and caused fatal obstruction.
  - **Acoustic disruption** — Low-frequency sonar and seismic surveys interfere with the Sperm Whale's own sophisticated echolocation system, causing disorientation and navigation failure.
  - **Bycatch** — Accidental entanglement in commercial fishing gear.
</Warning>

## Conservation solutions

<Tip>
  - All whale species in Thai waters are protected under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2535, with absolute prohibitions on hunting, trading, or possession.
  - Thailand's DMCR maintains response protocols specifically designed for large whale strandings and mass stranding events.
</Tip>

### Mass stranding response protocol

Sperm Whale mass strandings require rapid personnel management and coordination:

<Steps>
  <Step title="Triage living animals first">
    Prioritize animals that are still alive. Support each individual in the water to reduce stress and relieve compression on internal organs.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Protect blowhole, eyes, and skin">
    Never cover or allow water into the blowhole. Shield eyes from sand and direct sunlight. Apply wet cloths and pour water continuously to keep skin moist and reduce body temperature.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Coordinate pod release together">
    Because Sperm Whales maintain strong social ties, attempt to guide the entire pod back to deep water simultaneously. If a mother-calf pair is involved, keep them together — separating them increases the chance of the individuals returning to shore.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Do not delay">
    Large body mass causes rapid organ compression when a whale is out of water. Response speed directly determines survival outcomes.
  </Step>
</Steps>
