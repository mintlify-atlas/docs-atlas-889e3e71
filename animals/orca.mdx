## Introduction

The Killer Whale — commonly called the Orca — is the ocean's apex predator. Its body is powerfully built with a rounded head, a short beak, and large paddle-shaped pectoral fins. The dorsal fin is the Orca's most recognizable feature: in males it can reach 2 metres in height and stands upright; in females it is shorter and curves backward. Inside the mouth are 10–14 pairs of large, round-tipped, slightly curved teeth on both the upper and lower jaws.

Orcas hunt cooperatively in pods and feed on a wide range of prey, including fish, squid, marine mammals, and sea turtles. They are a natural predator of several other species documented in Polygon Animals — including Dugongs, sea turtles, and Manta Rays — and their presence in an area can cause prey animals to panic and flee into shallow water, sometimes leading to stranding.

All cetaceans — including Orcas — found in Thai waters are protected under national wildlife law.

## Threats and ecological role

<Warning>
  The Orca itself faces limited direct threats in Thai waters, but its predatory behavior has documented indirect effects on other protected species:

  - **Prey-driven strandings** — When Orcas hunt Dugongs, sea turtles, or Manta Rays, prey animals flee rapidly into shallow coastal zones. The sudden change in water pressure can cause decompression sickness, and the animals may become stranded.
  - **Disorientation** — Prey bitten or injured during a hunt may beach themselves due to shock or physical trauma.

  As an apex predator, the Orca is a keystone species: its presence structures the behavior of many other animals in Thai marine ecosystems.
</Warning>

## Conservation solutions

<Tip>

</Tip>

### Large cetacean stranding protocol

If an Orca or other large whale strands alive:

1. **Keep it upright** — Support the animal in the water to maintain normal posture and reduce pressure on internal organs. Never allow water to enter the blowhole.
2. **If on land** — Dig channels beneath the pectoral fins to relieve pressure. Use wet cloths to cover the body and shield the eyes from sand and sun. Keep the blowhole area clear at all times.
3. **Mass stranding management** — Orca strandings may involve entire pods. When a pod strands, immediate organization of personnel is required to support all individuals simultaneously. The goal is to guide the entire group back to deep water together — separating a pod can cause individuals to return to shore.
