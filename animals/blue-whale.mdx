## Introduction

The Blue Whale is the largest animal on Earth — the largest creature known to have ever existed. Its streamlined, elongated body is a distinctive blue-grey on the back and sides, with pale blue or light grey mottled spots distributed across the dorsal surface and flanks. The belly is slightly paler. The head is broad and U-shaped when viewed from above, with a single prominent ridge running along the rostrum.

Blue Whales feed almost exclusively on krill, filtering enormous quantities of water through their baleen plates. Despite their size, they are vulnerable to the same threats that affect smaller cetaceans, including bycatch, vessel strikes, and acoustic disruption from human activity.

On May 12, 2022, Thailand's Committee on Wildlife Conservation and Protection voted to designate the Blue Whale as a **reserved protected species** (สัตว์ป่าสงวน) — the highest protection category available under Thai national law. This designation reflects the extreme rarity of the species in Thai waters and the need for maximum enforcement measures.

## Threats

<Warning>
  Despite the absence of specific documented fatal incidents in Thai waters, Blue Whales are exposed to the same threats affecting all large cetaceans in the region:

  - **Bycatch** — Accidental entanglement in commercial fishing gear, particularly in areas where feeding grounds overlap with fishing activity.
  - **Plastic and pollution** — Ingestion of plastic waste and bioaccumulation of toxins from wastewater through the food chain.
  - **Acoustic pollution** — Petroleum exploration seismic surveys and vessel sonar disrupt long-distance communication and navigation, and can cause stranding.
  - **Vessel strikes** — Collisions with ship hulls or propeller damage.
  - **Climate and weather** — Rapid environmental change affects prey availability and migration patterns.
</Warning>

## Conservation solutions

<Tip>
  Thailand has taken significant legal action to protect the Blue Whale:

  - The 2022 **reserved wildlife** designation means the Blue Whale now holds equivalent status to Thailand's most iconic protected land animals. Any hunting, trade, or possession is a serious criminal offense.
  - The DMCR monitors sightings through online media and a citizen science network to build a database of status, habitat use, and migration routes — data that will inform future conservation planning.
  - Standardized stranding response protocols are in place. Trained response teams follow the DMCR manual for rare marine animal strandings to maximize survival chances if a Blue Whale is found injured or beached.
</Tip>

### Stranding response overview

In the unlikely event that a Blue Whale strands:

1. Due to the animal's extreme size, immediate coordination and personnel management are essential.
2. Support the animal in water if possible to reduce internal organ compression.
3. Wet cloths are used to keep the skin moist and reduce heat. The blowhole area is kept clear of water, sand, and covering materials at all times.
4. The goal is assessment and, where the animal is sufficiently healthy, guided return to deep water.
