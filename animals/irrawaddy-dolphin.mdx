## Introduction

The Irrawaddy Dolphin is immediately recognizable by its distinctive round head with no beak — a feature that sets it apart from most other dolphin species. Its back is blue-grey and its belly is lighter. The small, blunt dorsal fin sits in the middle of the body, slightly toward the tail.

This dolphin is a nearshore resident, meaning it lives and forages close to the coast rather than ranging into open ocean. Its preference for shallow, inshore habitats brings it into direct contact with fishing activity, coastal development, and marine pollution.

Globally, the Irrawaddy Dolphin is listed as Endangered on the IUCN Red List and placed on CITES Appendix I — the highest level of international protection — indicating extreme risk of extinction. In Thailand, the species has been elevated to reserved protected status under national wildlife law.

## Threats

<Warning>
  **Human-caused threats:**
  - **Coastal structures** — Wave-break bamboo fences and other inshore engineering can trap dolphins feeding in shallow water during low tide. A documented mass stranding of five Irrawaddy Dolphins occurred along a bamboo wave-break fence in Samut Sakhon province.
  - **Bycatch** — Accidental entanglement in fishing gear (gill nets, illegal nets) causes injury, disability, and death.
  - **Illegal fishing** — Blast fishing and prohibited gear in inshore areas.
  - **Marine pollution** — Plastic ingestion causes digestive obstruction; wastewater introduces toxins that accumulate in the body. Acoustic pollution from vessel traffic and sonar disrupts navigation and communication, causing disorientation and stranding.

  **Natural threats:**
  - **Navigation errors** — Pod-level behavior can lead entire groups into shallow water while chasing prey. If the lead dolphin misjudges depth, mass stranding can follow.
  - **Natural illness and age-related weakness.**
</Warning>

## Conservation solutions

<Tip>
  - The Irrawaddy Dolphin is protected under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act, with absolute prohibitions on hunting, trading, or possession.
  - CITES Appendix I listing blocks all international commercial trade.
  - When stranding is reported, trained teams follow the DMCR standard protocol for cetacean rescue.
</Tip>

### Stranding response

<Steps>
  <Step title="Stabilize in water">
    Support the dolphin in the water so it can maintain an upright position. Keep the blowhole on the top of the head clear and above the water surface at all times. Never cover the blowhole.
  </Step>
  <Step title="If ashore">
    Dig shallow depressions under the pectoral fins to reduce pressure on internal organs. Cover the body with a wet cloth, leaving the blowhole and dorsal fin uncovered. Continuously pour water over the animal to keep the skin moist and reduce body heat.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Transport">
    Use a correctly sized canvas stretcher with carrying poles to avoid additional injury during movement. Transfer to a rehabilitation pool or to a safe release site.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Veterinary care">
    Injured or ill dolphins are kept in a rehabilitation pool under close veterinary supervision until assessed as ready for release.
  </Step>
</Steps>
